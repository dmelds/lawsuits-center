Lawsuit Defense Guide

What a default judgment is and why deadlines matter.

A default judgment is a court ruling against a defendant who has failed to respond to a lawsuit on time. This page explains the basics — what it is, how it happens, what it can do, and what defendants typically do to address one.

How default judgments happen.

When someone is sued, they have a deadline to respond — usually stated in the summons. If the defendant does not file an answer or other proper response by that deadline, the plaintiff can ask the court to enter a default. The court may then enter a default judgment, awarding what the plaintiff requested (subject to certain limits and proof of damages).

Defaults can also occur after a case is underway — for example, if a party fails to appear at a required hearing, fails to comply with court orders, or stops participating in the case altogether.

What a default judgment can do.

A default judgment is a real, enforceable court order. Depending on the case, it can lead to wage garnishment, bank account levies, liens on real property, damage to credit, and other collection actions. In some cases, plaintiffs use default judgments to collect on debts the defendant disputes or never received proper notice of.

Default judgments can also have collateral consequences — affecting professional licenses, security clearances, or future credit applications — depending on the underlying claim and how the judgment is reported.

Setting aside a default.

Courts can sometimes set aside a default or default judgment, particularly if the defendant moves quickly and shows good cause — such as improper service, excusable neglect, fraud, or a meritorious defense. Standards and timelines vary significantly by jurisdiction, and some grounds (like void judgments for lack of service) have different rules than others.

The longer a default judgment sits, the harder it generally is to undo. Some states have firm outer deadlines for relief, after which only narrow grounds remain available.

Why response deadlines are critical.

The simplest way to avoid a default judgment is to respond on time. Even a basic answer that denies the allegations and raises defenses can preserve the right to fight the case on the merits.

If you have been served with a lawsuit and the deadline is approaching, contacting an attorney quickly is often more important than figuring out the right response on your own. Many defense attorneys offer initial consultations and can help evaluate options before the deadline expires.
